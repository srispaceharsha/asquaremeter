Week 9: Struggle is real!

March 01, 2026

Slow is smooth, smooth is good

Hello!

I crouch near the square meter plot, and notice all that moves. Carpenter ants, acrobat ants, few beetles, flies and wasps. All creating a constant sense of movement. They move with a sense of urgency and a goal.

Now I try practicing the art of "notice and let go" every time I spot a known bug. A temporary sense of familiarity kicks in. Now I am primed to notice unfamiliar things.

Every now and then, I spot an unfamiliar form moving about. My senses spring to action. I keep an eye on this bug as I move the camera and get ready to take a pic or two. Inevitably first few shots are shaky, unfocused and unusable.

I steady myself, hold my breath and let go of tension. I move along with the bug as I try to figure out what kind of bug it is. Is it moving in a predictable pattern? Can I anticipate where it will be in the next second? Or is it moving erratically that I have to resort to brute-forcing?

At this point, I am simply reacting to the movements of the bug. By now, I know this. Black Corinomma something spider. I have seen this before. It moves quite quickly and always takes the path through the grass and the undergrowth. Hoping it would hop onto a leaf and sitting still ain't gonna happen. I have to be really quick, steady and smooth.

Through the years of backyard wildlife photography, I kinda have an intuition if the shot I clicked is decent or not. I sense the images haven't come out well and a sense of urgency and frustration builds up, as the bug is moving near the border of the square meter plot.

Counter-intuitively, I have to slow down to take a decent shot rather than work myself up through this frustration.

The spider paused for ever so brief a moment. This is my chance. Perhaps the only chance I have today.

Click.

I think I got it. One more just to make sure I have it.

Here we go. Black Corinnomma Spider (Corinnomma severum).

I let the bug go on its way. I check the photos and I smile.

’Til next week!
