Moon Phase Methodology

This project records the moon phase at the time of each sighting. Here's how that works and what it means.

What We Record

For every sighting, I note the moon phase on that calendar day. That's it. I'm not claiming the moon makes bugs appear or disappear. I'm just writing down what phase it was when I saw something.

Correlation is not causation. If more sightings happen during a Waxing Gibbous moon, it could be random. Or maybe those days had better weather. Or I had more free time. The data shows when things happened together, nothing more.

How Phases Are Calculated

I use a Python library called PyEphem to calculate moon phases. For each date, it figures out:

  1. How much of the moon is illuminated (0% = new moon, 100% = full moon)
  2. Whether the moon is waxing (getting brighter) or waning (getting dimmer)
  3. How many days until the next new moon or full moon

These numbers determine which of the eight traditional phases applies to that day.

Phase Definitions

The eight phases are defined like this:

Phase Condition
New Moon Within 1 day of astronomical new moon
Waxing Crescent Waxing, illumination below 50%
First Quarter Waxing, illumination between 50% and 55%
Waxing Gibbous Waxing, illumination above 55%
Full Moon Within 1 day of astronomical full moon
Waning Gibbous Waning, illumination above 55%
Last Quarter Waning, illumination between 45% and 55%
Waning Crescent Waning, illumination below 45%

Why Some Phases Have Fewer Days

You might notice that First Quarter and Last Quarter show very few days in the statistics. This confused me too at first. Turns out it's just how the moon works.

The moon's illumination doesn't change at a constant rate. It moves slowly near new moon and full moon, but zips through the middle range quickly. The "quarter" phases, when illumination is around 50%, last only a day or two because the moon passes through that window fast.

Example: In January 2026, First Quarter happened at 4:47 AM on January 26th. By that evening, illumination had already crossed 55%, pushing the classification into Waxing Gibbous. The entire First Quarter window lasted less than one calendar day.

So the uneven day counts per phase are mathematically correct, even if they look odd.

Reading the Data

When you look at "Sightings by Moon Phase" on the statistics page, keep a few things in mind:

  • The number in parentheses shows how many days fell into that phase so far
  • More days in a phase means more chances to see things
  • If you want to compare activity levels fairly, think about sightings per day, not raw totals
  • We're still early in the project. Patterns might appear or vanish as the year goes on
