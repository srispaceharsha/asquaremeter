Moon Phase Methodology

This project records the moon phase at the time of each sighting. This page explains how the phases are calculated and what the data represents.

What We Record

For every sighting, we record the moon phase on that calendar day. This is purely observational data—we're documenting when creatures were observed, not claiming any lunar influence on their behavior.

Correlation is not causation. If more sightings occur during a Waxing Gibbous moon, it could mean many things: perhaps those days had better weather, or the observer had more time, or it's simply random variation. The data shows co-occurrence, nothing more.

How Phases Are Calculated

Moon phases are computed using PyEphem, an astronomical library. For each date, we calculate:

  1. The moon's illumination percentage (0% = new moon, 100% = full moon)
  2. Whether the moon is waxing (growing brighter) or waning (growing dimmer)
  3. The distance in days from the nearest new moon and full moon

These values determine which of the eight traditional phases applies.

Phase Definitions

The eight phases are defined as follows:

Phase Condition
New Moon Within 1 day of astronomical new moon
Waxing Crescent Waxing, illumination < 50%
First Quarter Waxing, illumination 50–55%
Waxing Gibbous Waxing, illumination > 55%
Full Moon Within 1 day of astronomical full moon
Waning Gibbous Waning, illumination > 55%
Last Quarter Waning, illumination 45–55%
Waning Crescent Waning, illumination < 45%

Why Some Phases Have Fewer Days

You may notice that First Quarter and Last Quarter have very few days compared to other phases. This is not a bug—it's astronomy.

The moon's illumination changes non-linearly through its ~29.5 day cycle. It moves slowly near the extremes (new and full) and quickly through the middle range. The "quarter" phases—when illumination crosses 50%—last only 1–2 days because the moon passes through that range rapidly.

For example: In January 2026, First Quarter occurred at 4:47 AM on January 26th. By that evening, illumination had already exceeded 55%, pushing the classification into Waxing Gibbous. The entire First Quarter "window" lasted less than a calendar day.

This is why the statistics show uneven day counts per phase. It's mathematically accurate, even if it seems counterintuitive.

Interpreting the Data

When viewing "Sightings by Moon Phase" on the statistics page, keep in mind:

  • The number in parentheses shows how many days fell into that phase during the project period
  • More days in a phase naturally means more opportunity for sightings
  • To compare activity levels, consider sightings per day of each phase, not raw totals
  • Sample sizes are still small—patterns may emerge or disappear as the year progresses
